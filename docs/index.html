Downtown Providence Parking

Providence has too much parking. I counted.

This is a map of all the parking in Downtown Providence. For the purposes of this page, “Downtown” refers to this area, adapted from official sources.

You can open the above map in a new tab.

This page is generated from OpenStreetMap data, much of which was added by me. Therefore, these counts aren’t official, and are definitely prone to error. If you see a data error and feel up for fixing it on OSM, eventually the change will be reflected here. This page was last updated on 21 January, 2020.

Street Parking

On-street parking occupies valuable public space that could be used for transit-only lanes, wider sidewalks, cycling tracks, trees, and other wonderful things. When street parking is free in dense, mixed-use urban areas, numerous problems arise.

There are approximately 2,163 on-street parking spaces. Some are metered or otherwise restricted, others are not. In some areas, like on Eddy Street behind City Hall, people regularly park on sidewalks.

If we approximate the dimensions of the average on-street parking space as 17 feet by 9 feet, we find that on-street parking accounts for 0.0119 miles2. The total area of the Downtown neighborhood is 0.78 miles2, so street parking accounts for approximately 1.53% of the Downtown land area.

Surface Parking

“Surface parking” refers to parking lots that are exclusively on ground level. When they are clustered together in urban areas they form parking craters just waiting to be filled in.

There are roughly 8,135 spaces in 188 surface lots. The average capacity of surface lots is 43.3, and the median capacity is 27.

Surface lots, combined, have a surface area of 249,228.96 ft2 (0.0962 mi2), which translate into roughly 12.35% of the Downtown land area. The average area of these is 1,325.69 ft2, and the median is 740.52 ft2.

Unlike on-street parking, which is typically parallel and relatively space-efficient, parking lots often have inner service roads or lanes, so that cars have space to turn around and maneuver. Given the total area and number of spaces, we can calculate that each individual parking space is responsible for an area of 329.77 ft2. If the average spot is assumed to be 17 feet × 9 feet = 153 ft2, each surface space accounts for an additional 176.77 ft2 (115.54%) for service roads, turn-around space, etc.

Note: some commuter lots have notably higher space efficiencies because they block cars in.

OpenStreetMap has a capacity:disabled tag to indicate the number of spaces which are considered (in the US), to be handicapped-accessible. I’ve done my best to catalogue these, but paint or signs indicating this in many lots is faded or out of date, so counts may be notably low. That said, the current data on OpenStreetMap suggests that there are 328 of these accessible spaces in 56 surface lots, approximately 4.03% of the total count of surface lots spaces. Lots with any disabled spaces have an average of 2.93 accessible spaces, and all surface lots have 0.87 accessible spaces on average.

Structured Parking

“Structured parking” refers to multi-story, above-ground parking garages, indicated by the OpenStreetMap multi-storey tag. They are more space-efficient than surface lots (though they are still hugely space-inefficient). Some in Providence include street-level retail and decorative facades, which mask some of the disappointing reality of upper levels. These design choices introduce some qualitative factors into space efficiency considerations, which I will not explore at present.

There are roughly 12,224 spaces in 25 structured lots. The average capacity of structured lots is 1,018, and the median is 525.

Structured lots, combined, have a surface area of 1,191,374.71 ft2. The average structured lot area is 47,654.99 ft2, with a median of 29,580.67 ft2.

If we calculate the average area per space, we find that it is 97.46 ft2, 70.45% lower than the average space required for a surface space.

Note: I’m not endorsing replacing all our surface parking with structured parking in the name of efficiency and capacity increases. But I’d much rather have one well-designed parking garage with 500 spaces than 15 surface lots with 500 spaces.

33 accessible spaces in structured garages have been indicated in the OpenStreetMap data. However, several large lots (like the Mall) definitely have accessible spaces but have not been included yet. Thus, it is not advisable to draw any conclusions based on this figure at present.

Underground Parking

Unsurprisingly, this refers to garages that are entirely underground.

There are roughly 1,051 spaces in 9 underground lots. The average capacity of underground lots is 210.2, and the median is 160.

While some area is used for entrance/exit ramps to these garages, it is negligible and will not be considered.

0 accessible underground spaces have been indicated in the OpenStreetMap data. However, several large lots may have accessible spaces, but have not been included. Thus, it is not advisable to draw any conclusions based on this figure at present.
